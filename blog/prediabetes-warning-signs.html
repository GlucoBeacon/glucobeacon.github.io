Pre-Diabetes: 7 Early Warning Signs You Shouldn't Ignore

5 min read

Pre-diabetes usually has no obvious symptoms, which is exactly why it's worth knowing the subtle signs — increased thirst, unusual fatigue, darkened skin patches, and more — since it's often reversible with lifestyle changes, but only if caught before it progresses to Type 2 diabetes. Pre-diabetes affects a large share of adults, and most don't know they have it because it frequently causes no obvious symptoms at all.

These seven signs don't diagnose anything on their own, but if several apply to you, they're a reasonable prompt to ask your doctor for a simple blood test.

1. Increased thirst and more frequent urination

When blood sugar runs higher than normal, your kidneys work harder to filter and remove the excess glucose, pulling more fluid from your body in the process. That extra fluid loss triggers more frequent urination — and more thirst to compensate.

2. Fatigue that doesn't match your sleep

When your cells can't take up glucose efficiently due to developing insulin resistance, they're effectively starved for energy even though there's plenty of sugar in your bloodstream. The result is a specific kind of tiredness that rest doesn't fully fix.

3. Darkened patches of skin

A condition called acanthosis nigricans — dark, velvety patches of skin, often on the neck, armpits, or groin — is a well-documented visible marker of insulin resistance. It's one of the few pre-diabetes signs you can literally see, and it's worth mentioning to a doctor if you notice it.

4. Increased hunger, especially after eating

Insulin resistance can create a frustrating cycle: cells aren't absorbing glucose efficiently, so your body signals hunger again soon after a meal, even though you technically just ate enough.

5. Slow-healing cuts or frequent infections

Elevated blood sugar can affect circulation and immune response, which is part of why minor cuts, bruises, or infections may take longer than usual to heal or resolve when blood sugar is running consistently high.

6. Blurred vision that comes and goes

High blood sugar can pull fluid from the lenses of your eyes, temporarily affecting their ability to focus. Unlike a permanent vision change, this kind of blurriness tends to fluctuate with blood sugar levels — which is itself a clue worth mentioning to a doctor.

7. Family history and weight around the midsection

Not a symptom in the traditional sense, but a genuine risk signal: having a parent or sibling with Type 2 diabetes, or carrying more weight around the abdomen specifically, both meaningfully raise your risk — enough that many clinicians recommend screening based on these factors alone, even with zero symptoms present.

What to actually do about it

None of these signs are a diagnosis by themselves — plenty of them overlap with ordinary life (poor sleep, hot weather, getting older). But if two or three sound familiar, or you have a family history, the actual next step is simple and low-stakes: ask your doctor for an A1C or fasting glucose test. It's a single blood draw, and pre-diabetes caught early is genuinely one of the more reversible conditions in medicine — often responding well to modest changes in diet, activity, and weight before it ever becomes Type 2.
