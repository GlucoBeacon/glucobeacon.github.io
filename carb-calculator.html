Net Carbs

Formula: Net Carbs = Total Carbs − Fiber − (Sugar Alcohols ÷ 2). This is the most common convention, though how much a sugar alcohol actually affects your blood sugar varies by type and by person — if you use a CGM, your own logged data is the real answer.

What are net carbs, and why do they matter?

Not every gram of carbohydrate on a label hits your bloodstream the same way. "Net carbs" is an attempt to estimate the portion that actually does.

Fiber isn't digested

Your body can't break fiber down into glucose, so it doesn't raise blood sugar the way starch or sugar does — subtracting it gives a more honest picture of a food's real impact.

Sugar alcohols are partial

Ingredients like erythritol or xylitol are absorbed differently than regular sugar — typically less, but not zero, and it varies by type and by person.

Glycemic index adds context

Two foods with identical carb counts can raise blood sugar at very different speeds. GI tells you how fast, not just how much.

It's an estimate, not a promise

Net carbs is a useful planning tool, not a guarantee — the only way to know exactly how a food affects you is to check your own blood sugar after eating it.

Where our GI numbers actually come from

Every GI value in our 176-food database traces back to real clinical testing — published, peer-reviewed tables, not a guess. For mixed dishes that were never lab-tested as a whole (nobody has tested "chicken enchiladas" — recipes vary too much), we won't invent a number. The honest method is a carb-weighted average of each ingredient's real GI, and even that documented technique runs 22–50% off a true lab-measured result — so we label composite estimates as estimates, not facts. A lot of GI tools skip this distinction entirely.
